Oculis Iuris

Oculis Iuris — Latin for "Eyes of the Law." We are the forensic medicine club of JSS Medical College, established to bridge the gap between clinical medicine and the justice system.

Forensic Medicine is the branch of medicine that applies medical knowledge to legal investigations. From identifying causes of death, interpreting injury patterns, to toxicology analysis — forensic physicians serve as the eyes of the justice system.

Our club exists to cultivate forensic curiosity among medical students. We believe every doctor should understand how medicine intersects with law — because the evidence you gather in a clinical setting may one day stand in a court of justice.

Through case discussions, workshops, seminars, and interactive learning, Oculis Iuris trains the next generation of forensic-aware physicians — students who see not just the wound, but the story behind it.

Case of the Month — AUTOPSY REPORT
The Case of the Month
AUTOPSY REPORT
OCULIS IURIS — DEPT. OF FORENSIC MEDICINE — JSS MEDICAL COLLEGE
Case No.OI-COTM-2025-03
Date of ExaminationMarch 2025
VictimMale, approx. 42 years
SceneLocked room, urban residential
Scene Description

Body of a 42-year-old male found in a closed room with windows sealed with tape. A small portable kerosene heater was found still running. There were no signs of struggle. Witnesses report the victim was last seen alive approximately 14 hours prior. The body appeared in a natural sleeping position on a cot.

Post-Mortem Findings
LividityFixed, posterior — consistent with position
Rigor MortisPresent in upper limbs, partially resolving in lower limbs
Skin ColorCherry-red discolouration of skin and mucous membranes
Blood AnalysisCarboxyhaemoglobin saturation elevated — 68%
Cause of Death████████ ████████ ██████████
INVESTIGATOR INPUT — What killed the victim?
Forensic Explanation

Cause of Death: Carbon Monoxide Poisoning.

The kerosene heater in a sealed room produced CO gas. CO binds to haemoglobin 250× more avidly than oxygen, forming carboxyhaemoglobin (COHb), causing tissue hypoxia despite adequate ambient oxygen.

Key forensic markers: (1) Cherry-red skin due to COHb, (2) COHb saturation of 68% — lethal (>50% is life-threatening), (3) No signs of struggle (victim was likely asleep when levels became lethal), (4) Sealed room consistent with CO accumulation.

Manner of death: Accidental. Weapon: Domestic heater in enclosed space.
